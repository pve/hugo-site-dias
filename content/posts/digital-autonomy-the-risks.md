When discussing digital autonomy, the information risks are the usual ones.
Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability.

Beyond that, there are governance related risks that reduce the capability of an actor (e.g. government) to govern and control.
This includes all means to influence other international actors, including military capability.


Let's have a look at the assets at risk, looking through the lens of sovereignty risks.


Confidentiality of internal government communication
Military capability
Capability to regulate businesses and individuals
Ability to fulfill responsibilities to citizens (laws, privacy rights)
Economic policy as a tool for development

License to operate and regulatory compliance
Assets and profits
Capability to compete, innovate, and develop

Capability to enforce rules by regulators

Capability to execute (NGOs)

Privacy rights (as data subjects)
Digital infrastructure broadly (guide-autonomy-resilience.md and how-can-we-achieve-autonomy.md)

Availability of IT services (email, file storage, operations)
Control over the supply chain (hardware, software, systems management, personnel)
Democracy and prosperity (framed as societal assets)

Then there is perception of loss of control.

Regulatory risk.

What risks are implied by -non-residency? It is the legal fiction that residency (in a foreigen location) implies control. It does not, at least not totally.
Residency does allow the state actor to 'cut the power'.
