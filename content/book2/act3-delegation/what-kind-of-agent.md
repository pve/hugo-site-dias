## Thesis

"Agent" is the most overloaded word of the AI wave.
The book already has a precise definition available: an agent is anything that can make and keep promises — human, organization, software, or AI.
Use that definition to sort the hype, and to see what is genuinely new about AI agents: their promises are broad, their self-knowledge is poor, and their autonomy is bounded by whoever runs their loop.

## What goes in

- The confusion, briefly: "agentic AI", "AI agents run your business" — half the world talks about it, definitions differ per vendor (harvest opening from the what-is-an-agent post).
- The spectrum of delegation, recapping the act so far: script (fixed instructions) → service (defined promises, unit 16) → agent (an intent, a loop, tools, and discretion in how to fulfil the promise).
- Promise-theory framing: what distinguishes an agent is not intelligence but the ability to voluntarily make promises about its own behavior; autonomy means promises can only be made about oneself.
- The self-awareness problem — the agent-self-aware anecdote as the centerpiece: the agent answered questions about its own configuration with confidence, but had no way to know; agents make assertions beyond their knowledge, fluently.
  Consequence: an agent's promises about itself deserve the least trust (link: identity is asserted, unit 11a; trickster figure per the Campbell review).
- The loop matters more than the model: observe/act cycles, tool access (MCP as the promise interface between agent and tools), and who is the principal — every agent acts on someone's behalf; name the principal and much confusion disappears.
- Attention as the scarce resource (the intell-is-attention idea stub, if it earns its place): intelligence directed by attention; delegation is buying attention.
- The practical test to give the reader: for any "agent" you meet, name (1) the promise, (2) the boundary it operates in, (3) the principal, (4) how the promise is verified.
  If you can't, you don't have an agent — you have a liability.
